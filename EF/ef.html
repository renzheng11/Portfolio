ELECTRIC FIELD

Before we begin looking into how transistors work, we first need to understand the electric field. In this chapter, we see how two charged surfaces interact with each other.

[SCENE 1]: 3D Field

Any positive or negative charge creates an electric field in the space surrounding it (typically represented by arrows). If we line up an array of electric fields, we have a screen (or sheet).

Click on the right graphic to charge it with electrons.

[SCENE 2]: Change Electrons

Here, we've simplified the graphic to show one array for visualization purposes.

If we have an array of positive charges, for the side of the sheet facing left, the direction of the electric field (on x plane) is negative. For the side of the sheet facing right, the direction of the electric field is positive. Try changing the number of electrons and see how it affects the electric field vector. Try changing the magnitude by dragging the arrows.

[SCENE 3]: Change Charge

What about an array of negative charges? What directions for the electric field would the left and right sides of the sheet be?

[SCENE 4]

For the side of the sheet facing left, the direction of the electric field is positive. For the side of the sheet facing right, the direction of the electric field is negative.

Let’s look at what happens when we put two sheets of opposite charges together. We can see that the left and right sides of the charges cancel each other except the middle section.

Drag your mouse to move the sheet to see the effect.

Use the button below to show the screening effect.

[SCENE 5]: Imperfect Screening

We put a negative sheet on the right side of the positive sheet. The negative charges “screen” (cancel) the positive charges. The positive charges screen the negative charges. That leaves us with an intersecting portion creates a doubled electric field that is of negative charge and doubled. (Try the slider and see what happens when you move the negative array). This effect is called screening.

[SCENE 6]: More Sheets

The process screening is not perfect. The strength of the electric charges in the two arrays may not match exactly, so the charges are not fully cancelled. If we have a negative array with less strength, the negative charge of the middle portion is still stronger but not doubled.

[SCENE 7]: Multiple Sheets

What if we try using multiple sheets? We can put multiple sheets, with charges of varying (strengths). Then the sheets will screen out the positive electric field over a distance.

[SCENE 9]: Volume

The negative charge is then distributed over a distance where eventually, total positive charge = total negative charge. The set of negative sheets can be considered a volume. The volume might be a sheet of metal that is a semiconductor.
